ferris wheel for sweets

bringing my ice cream cone ferris wheel to life

concept

Build a moving ferris wheel that holds ice cream cones and can be used at a fun event. For my particular use case, I wanted to use the chocolate ice cream cone molds from one of my previous projects.

Even though I knew what I wanted to build, I went to Pinterest to see if there was anything I could learn. Apparently, a lot of people make cupcake ferris wheels

technical implementation

Prerequisite

Working with existing components

Before jumping into anything, I had to take stock of existing components that I would be working off and ensure fit by measuring each thing with calipers. This included:

  • Dowel diameter: This would be used to hold all of the ferris wheel components together
  • Chocolate Cone diameter and height: To ensure it fit the seat and wouldn't hit the floor once the ferris wheel was put together

01

Building parametric CAD model with Rhino and Grasshopper

Leverage the ability to easily change measurements for pieces that need to fit together.

A few tips:

  • Group your work so it's easier to reference later
  • Remember to bake. I had a moment where I was exporting but nothing exported until I realized I forgot to bake my models
  • Bake to different layers, I find that this helps me keep my shapes more organized
  • Export as an Adobe Illustrator (.ai) file

For more details on how to use Rhino, check out Assignment 2

02

Building crank and booth seat CAD models with Onshape

Since I wanted to focus more on building a single, solid component, and ensure that it fit with existing pieces like the dowel and casted chocolate cones, I decided to use Onshape to build them.


I made sure to:
  • Label each layer with a specific name
  • Check and recheck dimensions. Since I was using the same stock dowel part as my previous project, I wanted to be sure I was using the same dimensions since it had such a great fit

3D print hand crank

Prepare the file

  • Export the file from Onshape as a DXF file
  • Open the file in the printer software. I use Cura for my Ultimaker printers
  • Make sure the size is maintained with a 1:1 millimeter transition
  • Save the file to a USB stick

Print

When printing, make sure to stay with the print until the first layer has successfully printed.

3D Print Settings and Details:

  • Infill: 40% (since I wanted it to be sturdier and have more heft)
  • No supports needed since there was always a stable layer underneath each top layer
  • Material: PLA

02

Laser cut

Before you go

To make sure everything fit, I brought the package of dowels and the 3D print of the original ice cream cone mold to see if the seat I made was a good fit.

  • Plan to wait for a lasercutter.
    It was about 1.5 hours before I finally got to cut.
  • Go from smaller pieces to larger pieces
    Instead of cutting out the ferris wheel first, I cut out the booths first since that would be the main component that I would need to work around from a sizing issue. After successfully laser cutting cardboard, I moved to wood to see how different the tolerances were.
  • Test on cheaper materials, like cardboard
    I ended up having to resize my cut downward after the first laser cut since the holes were too big because I forgot to translate my diamter measurement to a radius measurement from Onshape to Grasshopper.
  • Do a fit test immediately
    Make sure to test fit the pieces while you're near the laser cut to ensure everything fits together as intended
  • Reuse materials when possible
    Don't throw away the rounded triangle ferris wheel cut outs, you'll need about 6 of them later

Prepare the file and materials

  • Open the exported files in Adobe Illustrator including ferris wheel, stand, and booth sides from Rhino and the seat from Onshape (DXF file that can be opened in Illustrator)
  • Make sure it's imported in at a 1:1 millimeter ratio
  • Change stroke width to .001 pt. It will look barely visible on screen.
  • Put each element on its own layer to make editing easier when you laser cut. Sometimes, certain lines won't cut as well if the wood used to laser cut isn't flat. Instead of recutting everything, this gives you the flexibility to only cut that one area instead of everything.

Set-up print settings and laser settings

For more details on how to do this, check out Assignment 1

Print

Lasercut on cardboard first

Once the fit is established, lasercut on your actual material

Laser Cut Settings:

  • Thin Cardboard: 20% speed, 100% power, 50% frequency
  • Thick Cardboard: 10% speed, 100% power, 50% frequency
  • Wood: 6% speed, 100% power, 50% frequency

03

Cut and smooth dowels

Cut dowels to 1" pieces. I actually learned a few different tips around cutting dowels that were both pretty successful

Dowel Cutting Tips

  • There was someone in the Mill that shared this tip since I was using a mini saw and table clamp. If using a saw to cut the dowel, cut a notch on one side of the dowel and then cut from the other side to reduce splintering


  • Online, I learned that you can actually use dog nail cutting scissors to make a clean cut. I only used this tip once to cut off the extra part of my main ferris wheel pole, but it made SUCH a clean cut and was so easy to use. I kind of wish I did this for all the parts
  • I ended up using scissors to smooth out the rough edges

04

Put everything together

Remember to wipe off the excess ash from the laser cutter before starting

Build the booths
  • Set up your hot glue gun and glue sticks
  • Fit the sides of the seat to the bottom of the seat. Glue the edges for extra security.
  • Mark the sides of the booths to ensure the dowels are glued evenly to both sides. Use a pen to mark the middle of the seat to make sure it's balanced. (In hindsight, I should have made a mark for this while I was making the seat sides, so it's a design improvement for next time.)

  • Glue the dowels to the side of the seat
  • Set aside and let cool
Connect the ferris wheel + seats and test its turning capability
  • Put one side of the ferris wheel on a table on a slightly lifted surface in the middle. You can use your leftover triangles to create space
  • Connect the booths to the holes. Make sure all the seats are facing the same direction
  • Put the other side of the ferris wheel on top and make sure the booth is connected on both sides.
  • Spin the wheel to make sure it's turning and the seats are flipping downward. If any look stuck, you can use the CAD model of the ice cream cone used for the mold as a slight weight.
  • Hot glue one side of the ferris wheel to a full length dowel through it's center point
  • Move the other side until there's enough space so the seats aren't right up against the carousel since that will create more friction and make it hard for the seat to maintain its direction
  • Glue the other ferris wheel's center point to the dowel. Test and move as necessary. You may notice that the booths may fall out from one side or the other, which leads to our next step.
  • Create little caps for the dowels sticking out of the booth by putting a dab of hot glue on top of the pole and letting it dry. By making the width of the dowel increase, the sides of the booth can't fall out
Add the stand
  • Now add the triangles. You'll notice that if you put the triangles/stand right up to the ferris wheel, it will hit the sides of the booth and stop turning, so make sure there's space
  • Test by placing the ferris wheel in between the triangles on an even surface and test spinning
  • Once satisfied, add hot glue to the sides of the stand since we don't want it to move with the ferris wheel
Make stand supports
  • Take the leftover triangle pieces from the carousel and glue 2 sets with 3 triangles glued together in each set
  • Test where the supports should be before glueing to see which points add the most stability. The wood I used was slightly bent and warped, so I had to offcenter my supports
  • Both triangle sets will be in between the stands. Glue one set of 3 to one leg and one set of 3 to the other
Try spinning it again to see if it works as intended
